# Canonical Summary

> Public SAE, IAQG, IPC, FAR, and DLAD records are now strong enough to support a guarded aerospace-quality workflow layer in `llm_wiki`: `AS9102C` for first-article-inspection identity, IAQG `9102` for standardized cross-supply-chain `FAI` process and documentation context, IAQG `SCMH` guidance for the boundary that effective `FAI` programs may be self-imposed or contract-required rather than universally mandatory, `AS9145` for product-and-process-validation workflow identity, `AS9103` for variation-management vocabulary, IAQG `OASIS` plus the broader IAQG certification page for certification-record and oversight ecosystem context, IPC Validation Services for the separate idea that standards can coexist with formal validation or qualification-listing programs, `FAR 52.246-11` for contract-listed higher-level quality-standard and subcontract-flowdown wording, and `DLAD 46.291` for contract-governed production-lot-testing vocabulary. None of these public pages prove that a supplier executed a compliant package, passed an audit, or is qualified for a specific `22-layer` program.

## Stable Facts

- `AS9102C` remains the official identity anchor for aerospace first-article-inspection requirements.
- `AS9100D` publicly adds a baseline aerospace `QMS` layer by stating that its requirements are complementary to customer and applicable statutory/regulatory requirements, and that those outside requirements take precedence if they conflict.
- IAQG publicly frames `9102` as a standardized `FAI` process and documentation approach used across the aerospace, space, and defense supply chain.
- IAQG publicly ties that standardized `FAI` approach to more consistent verification and reduced variation/product-safety risk at supply-chain level.
- IAQG `SCMH` publicly frames `First Article Inspection` guidance as help for executing an effective `FAI` program rather than as a universal auditable requirement.
- IAQG `SCMH` also publicly states that `9102` may be self-imposed or contract-required, which makes `FAI` workflow language program-sensitive rather than globally mandatory by default.
- `AS9145` publicly identifies an aerospace workflow layer for advanced product quality planning and production part approval through product and process validation, and publicly links that layer to `9100`, `9102`, and `9103`.
- `AS9101G` publicly identifies a distinct audit-process layer for preparing, executing, and reporting audits of conformity and process effectiveness against `9100-series` standards, organizational `QMS` documentation, and customer plus statutory/regulatory requirements.
- `AS9103B` publicly identifies a variation-management layer for key characteristics across production processes, which supports guarded wording about continuing process discipline after first-article activity.
- `AS9131D` publicly identifies a distinct nonconformity-reporting layer in which supplier tiers and customers exchange defined nonconformity data/documentation when formal decision is required, with applicability that can be contract-driven.
- IAQG `OASIS` publicly frames certification and audit record checking as part of an official verification ecosystem used for supplier selection and surveillance.
- IAQG's certification page publicly shows that `OASIS` sits inside a broader certification and oversight ecosystem rather than standing alone as a generic supplier-search tool.
- IPC Validation Services publicly shows that electronics-industry governance can include structured validation, gap-analysis, and qualification-listing pathways in addition to standards naming alone.
- IPC's Validation Services FAQ publicly distinguishes `QPL` product/site qualification from `QML` manufacturer/process qualification, and publicly shows that qualification listings, audit roles, and site-scope boundaries are part of that ecosystem.
- IPC's Standards Gap Analysis page publicly shows that formal assessment and inconsistency-review workflows can sit before qualification/listing language enters the picture.
- IPC's public `QPL IPC-4101` page now makes it clearer that public qualification-listing vocabulary can attach to certified base materials and listed products/sites, not only to finished-board or manufacturer-process claims in the abstract.
- `FAR 44.303` publicly identifies a contractor purchasing-system review layer that can include subcontractor-responsibility evaluation, planning/award/postaward management of major subcontract programs, management-control systems including internal audit procedures, and implementation of higher-level quality standards.
- `FAR 52.246-11` publicly identifies a contract-clause layer in which higher-level quality standards are listed in the contract and applicable requirements may need to flow down to lower-tier subcontracts for critical/complex items or technically controlled work.
- `DLAD 46.291` publicly identifies a distinct contract-governed production-lot-testing layer in which lot-conformance evidence, reports, traceability documentation, and related authority boundaries can sit before lot acceptance.

- Use this card when a draft needs guarded wording such as `audit-process layer`, `conformity reporting`, or `process-effectiveness reporting` without implying that any supplier passed such an audit.
- Use this card when a draft needs guarded wording such as `customer flowdown takes precedence`, `baseline QMS layer`, or `contract-driven nonconformity reporting` without implying approval or release authority.
- Use this card when a draft needs guarded wording such as `site-specific qualification scope`, `public qualification listing`, or `pre-qualification gap-analysis workflow` without implying approval or release authority.
- Use this card when a draft needs guarded wording such as `purchasing-system review`, `subcontractor responsibility`, `postaward management`, or `higher-level quality-standards review` without implying that any supplier passed such a review.

## Limits And Non-Claims

- This card does not provide `FAI` form content, re-accomplishment triggers, or acceptance thresholds.
- It does not prove that any supplier performs `AS9102` correctly, maintains baseline packages, passed an aerospace audit, completed `PPAP`, or is listed in `OASIS`.
- It does not prove that any supplier passed an `AS9101` audit, satisfies `9100-series` audit expectations, or achieved acceptable process-effectiveness results for a specific `22-layer` program.
- It does not prove that any supplier conforms to `AS9100`, received customer approval under `AS9131`, or has authority to disposition nonconforming `22-layer` product.
- It does not prove that a supplier is validated, qualified, listed, or trusted under any IPC Validation Services pathway.
- It does not prove that an `IPC-4101` `QPL` listing qualifies a finished board, interconnect structure, or customer program.
- It does not prove that any supplier passed a contractor purchasing-system review, implemented higher-level quality standards acceptably, or is suitable for a specific government or aerospace `22-layer` program.
- It does not prove that `FAR 52.246-11` or `DLAD 46.291` was invoked in a specific procurement, nor does it identify the exact higher-level standard, sample plan, or lot-test method for a specific `22-layer` board.
- It does not make IPC gap analysis, `QPL`, or `QML` equivalent to customer approval, aerospace qualification, or release authority for a specific `22-layer` board.
- It does not make `FAI` universally mandatory for every `22-layer` board.
