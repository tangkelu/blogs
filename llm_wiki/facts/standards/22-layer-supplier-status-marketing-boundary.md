# Canonical Summary

> Public IAQG, IPC Validation Services, and DLA qualification-ecosystem records are strong enough to support guarded `22-layer` governance vocabulary around certification infrastructure, listing frameworks, audit ecosystems, and qualification hierarchies. They are not strong enough to prove that any `22-layer` supplier is approved, trusted, listed, certified, qualified, or otherwise program-accepted.

## Stable Facts

- A conservative `22-layer` rewrite may describe supplier-governance ecosystems as public context rather than supplier-status proof.
- Public `AS9100D` metadata supports baseline `QMS` vocabulary under customer and regulatory precedence, not approved-supplier status.
- Public `AS9101G` metadata supports audit-process vocabulary, not acceptable audit outcomes.
- Public `AS9131D` metadata supports formal nonconformity-reporting vocabulary, not waiver authority or release authority.
- Public IAQG `OASIS` and certification pages support certification-record and oversight-framework vocabulary, not customer approval.
- Public IPC Validation Services pages support validation, gap-analysis, and listing-ecosystem vocabulary, not finished-board supplier approval.
- Public `QML IPC-1791` and `QML J-STD-001S` pages support organization-level or assembly-process listing vocabulary, not rigid-board supplier approval for a `22-layer` product.
- Public `MIL-PRF-31032` and DLA `QML/QPL/QBL` hierarchy pages support qualification-ecosystem framing, not current acceptable-supplier status.
- These sources together support guarded wording such as `public governance ecosystem`, `listing framework`, `oversight layer`, and `site-specific scope`, but not `approved supplier`.

## Conditions And Methods

- Use this card when a `22-layer` draft risks turning public ecosystem vocabulary into `trusted supplier`, `qualified source`, `approved manufacturer`, or similar marketing claims.
- Pair this card with `facts/standards/22-layer-qualification-listing-and-release-authority-boundary.md`, `facts/standards/22-layer-high-reliability-rewrite-guardrail.md`, and `facts/standards/22-layer-contract-flowdown-and-lot-conformance-boundary.md`.
- Prefer wording such as `sits in a public ecosystem`, `may appear in`, `does not prove approval by itself`, and `requires separate supplier evidence`.
- Keep certification, listing, validation, audit, and military-qualification references attached to role/scope boundaries rather than supplier-status conclusions.

## Limits And Non-Claims

- This card does not prove that any `22-layer` supplier is approved, certified, listed, validated, trusted, or acceptable to a specific customer or program.
- It does not prove that any supplier passed an audit, holds a current listing, or is authorized to release product.
- It does not turn `OASIS`, IPC Validation Services, or DLA infrastructure vocabulary into accepted-supplier proof.
- It does not provide supplier-evidence, current certificate status, site-specific scope, or customer-approval evidence.

## Open Questions

- Add a supplier-evidence layer only if future work truly needs manufacturer-specific approval or listing claims.
- Reassess `22-layer` readiness only after supplier-status wording is fully contained from prompt retrieval.
